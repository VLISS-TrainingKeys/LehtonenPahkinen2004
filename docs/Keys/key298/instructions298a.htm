INSTRUCTIONS FOR TRAINING KEY 298:

ACCOUNTING FOR CLUSTERING EFFECTS FOR A CONTINOUS STUDY VARIABLE

The aim in this part is to compare empirically methods that aim at accounting for sampling design complexities: stratification, clustering and weighting. The main concern is in clustering effects.

We compare empirically the methods using the OHC Survey dataset. We use perceived psychic strain (PSYCH) as the variable of interest.

The continuous variable PSYCH is the first principal component of nine psychic symptoms (explaining 42 % of the total variation of the symptoms). The variable PSYCH is standardized for mean zero and variance one. Psychic strain increases with increasing the value of PSYCH. The overall design effect of PSYCH is about 2 indicating substantial clustering effect.

Our main explanatory variable is CHRON (Chronic morbidity). Age and sex are used as additional predictors in the models; we calculate sex-age adjusted figures.

For the continuous study variable PSYCH we carry out further use of the SUDAAN procedure REGRESS and SAS procedure SURVEYREG. We compare the SUDAAN and SAS results with those given by the SAS procedures GENMOD and MIXED, and the R functions gee and lme.

In the previous Training Keys we have used SUDAAN analysis procedures to account for the clustering effects. Modeling based on pseudo-maximum likelihood (PML) estimation has been this far our main method. We now turn to certain alternative methods.

METHODS WE DEMONSTRATE ARE THE FOLLOWING:

- Modeling for a fixed-effects linear model based on generalized estimating equations (GEE) techniques with an exchangeable correlation structure, using the SUDAAN procedure REGRESS, the SAS procedure GENMOD and the R function gee.

- Linear mixed modeling based on restricted maximum likelihood (REML) estimation, using the SAS procedure MIXED and the R function lme. We use a simple model involving cluster-specific random intercepts in addition to the fixed effects (a variance components model).

In all these instances, standard errors of model coefficients are estimated by a robust (or empirical), “sandwich” type estimator (Lehtonen and Pahkinen 2004 page 271).

A standard ML estimation procedure by the SAS procedure REG, using a model-based standard error estimate, is used as a reference. All sampling complexities are ignored under this option.

Analysis options applied are the following:

Option Procedure Accounting for…
--------------------------------------------------------------------------
Option 1a: SUDAAN/REGRESS Clustering and stratification
Option 1b: SAS/SURVEYREG Clustering and stratification
Option 2a:    SAS/MIXED Clustering
Option 2b: R - lme Clustering
Option 3a SAS/GENMOD Clustering
Option 3b: R - gee Clustering
Option 4: SAS/REG None (reference option)

NOTE! In MIXED and GENMOD procedures and in R functions, the variable CLUSTER is transformed to a new variable SUBJECT having values 1,2,...,250.

GEE estimation and computation are discussed in Diggle, Liang and Zeger (1994) and Horton and Lipsitz (1999). Mixed, or multilevel, models are addressed in Goldstein (1995) and McCulloch and Searle (2001). Multilevel modeling is also discussed in Chapter 9 of Lehtonen and Pahkinen (2004).
