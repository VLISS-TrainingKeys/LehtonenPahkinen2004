INSTRUCTIONS FOR TRAINING KEY 298:

ACCOUNTING FOR CLUSTERING EFFECTS FOR A BINARY STUDY VARIABLE      

 

The aim in this part is to compare empirically methods that aim at accounting for sampling design complexities: stratification, clustering and weighting. The main concern is in clustering effects.

We compare empirically the methods using the OHC Survey dataset. We use perceived psychic strain (PSYCH) as the variable of interest.

For the binary study variable, people having PSYCH score greater than zero are included in the higher strain group (value 1); a value of zero is assigned otherwise. The overall design effect of PSYCH2 is 1.7 indicating substantial clustering effect.

Our main explanatory variable is CHRON (Chronic morbidity). Age and sex are used as additional predictors in the models; we calculate sex-age adjusted figures.

For the binary study variable PSYCH2 we carry out further use of the SUDAAN procedure LOGISTIC (RLOGIST) and SAS procedure SURVEYLOGISTIC. We compare the SUDAAN and SAS results with those given by the SAS procedure GENMOD, the SAS macro GLIMMIX, and the R functions gee and glmmPQL.

In the previous Training Keys we have used SUDAAN analysis procedures to account for the clustering effects. Modeling based on pseudo-maximum likelihood (PML) estimation has been this far our main method. We now turn to certain alternative methods.

METHODS WE DEMONSTRATE INCLUDE THE FOLLOWING:

- Modeling for a fixed-effects logistic model based on generalized estimating equations (GEE) techniques with an exchangeable correlation structure, using the SUDAAN procedure LOGISTIC(RLOGIST), the SAS procedure GENMOD and the R function gee.

- Logistic mixed modeling based on restricted maximum likelihood (REML) estimation, using the SAS macro GLIMMIX and the R function glmmPQL. We use a simple model involving cluster-specific random intercepts in addition to the fixed effects (a variance components model).

In all these instances, standard errors of model coefficients are estimated by a robust (or empirical), “sandwich” type estimator (Lehtonen and Pahkinen 2004 page 271).

A standard ML estimation procedure by the SAS procedure GENMOD, using a model-based standard error estimate, is used as a reference. All sampling complexities are ignored under this option.

Analysis options applied are the following:

Option Procedure Accounting for…
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Option 1a: SUDAAN/RLOGIST Clustering and stratification
Option 1b: SAS/SURVEYLOGISTIC Clustering and stratification
Option 2a: SAS/GLIMMIX Clustering
Option 2b: R/glmmPQL Clustering
Option 3a: SAS/GENMOD Clustering
Option 3b: R/gee Clustering
Option 4: SAS/GENMOD None (reference option)

NOTE! In GLIMMIX macro and GENMOD procedure, and in R functions, the variable CLUSTER is transformed to a new variable SUBJECT having values 1,2,...,250.

GEE estimation and computation are discussed in Diggle, Liang and Zeger (1994) and Horton and Lipsitz (1999). Mixed, or multilevel, models are addressed in Goldstein (1995) and McCulloch and Searle (2001). Multilevel modeling is also discussed in Chapter 9 of Lehtonen and Pahkinen (2004).
