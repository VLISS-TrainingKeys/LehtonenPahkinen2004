TRAINING KEY 45 Part B: Summary of Intra-Class Correlation Coefficients and DEFFs produced by varying the sorting order of the Province'91 population

Systematic sampling can be very efficient if the sorting order used generates internally heterogeneous sampling intervals such that the interval-specific means of the study variable are approximately equal. This can be achieved if the auxiliary variable used in sorting correlates strongly with the study variable. We will examine four different sorting orders of the Province'91 population when a systematic sampling scheme with N=32, n=8 and sampling interval q=4 is applied.

In (a), we use the same sorting order as in Part A. Calculated approximative DEFF=0.426 indicates that the list order of the municipalities (sorting first by URB85 and then by the name of the municipality) is interestingly associated with the number of unemployed, UE91. In (b) and (c), we use HOU85 as sorting variable. Note that the variable HOU85 correlates strongly with the study variable UE91. In (b), the population of municipalities is sorted in descending order by HOU85. Surprisingly, the design effect is DEFF=0.531 which results in less efficient systematic sampling as in case (a). Again in (c) we start with descending order of HOU85 and in addition we introduce an "optimal" permutation of units within sampling intervals. This results in DEFF=0.329. As a reference in (d), where we aim at a randomly ordered population with respect to the study variable UE91, any substantial gain cannot be expected, as indicated by a design effect close to one and systematic sampling is almost equally efficient as SRSWOR.

We next illustrate in more detail the effect of the sorting order on the efficiency of systematic sampling. The next table gives the means of the study variable for each sample configuration under the four sorting orders. It can be clearly seen that in (c) the means are closest to each other and therefore, the intra-cluster correlation is the smallest.

Note that the intra-class correlation coefficient is negative in all example cases which means that given the different sorting orders examined, systematic sampling in these cases is more efficient than simple random sampling without replacement (SRSWOR).

Note also that results in (d) depend on the choice of the seed number for the generation of the sequence of random numbers. This is because we now have no control over the composition of the sampling intervals and our population size happens to be small. In this example the seed is 654326597 and it produces a DEFF close to one. If a different seed number is chosen, different results will be obtained (see the table below).
